# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Recruiters and hiring managers scanning many developer portfolios. They're time-constrained, evaluating credibility and fit quickly. Secondary audience: potential collaborators and fellow builders who dig into the work more carefully. Both need a fast first impression and a payoff if they keep reading.

## Product Purpose

Brandon's personal portfolio. It exists to help him land a software engineering or data science role by making him memorable, credible, and distinct among a field of similar candidates. Success means a recruiter finishes the page with a clear sense of who he is, what he's built, and where he's going — and wants to reach out.

## Brand Personality

Ambitious, earnest, building. This person is clearly going somewhere. The portfolio should feel like evidence of motion: not just what he's done, but what he cares about and what he's working toward. The tone is confident without posturing — someone who takes their craft seriously without performing seriousness.

## Anti-references

- **Generic dark SaaS dev portfolio**: purple or teal on deep charcoal, uniform card grid, floating particles, identical uppercase eyebrow labels on every section. The current design is in this lane and needs to move.
- **Corporate clean / safe**: neutral grays, polished but personality-free. Looks like it was made from a LinkedIn template.
- **Editorial-magazine**: italic display serif, Klim-font affectation, ruled separators, broadsheet-column layout. Design-forward but wrong register for a dev portfolio.
- **Brutalist / maximalist**: chaotic layout, experimental type for its own sake, "look how weird I am" energy. Undermines credibility with recruiters.
- **Stripe imitation**: the energy reference is Stripe's discipline and reader-respect, not its specific aesthetic. Don't copy the exact palette or layout; draw from the principle.

## Design Principles

1. **Earn the reader's time.** Recruiters scan. Hierarchy should tell the full story in under 10 seconds; the rest rewards those who keep reading.
2. **Specificity is the brand.** Brandon isn't "a developer." He co-founded a robotics club at a liberal arts college, built an ML diagnostic tool at 96% accuracy, and is pursuing systems programming to understand how runtimes work. The design should make those specifics visible, not bury them in generic descriptions.
3. **Show trajectory, not just history.** The Future Plans section is genuinely unusual in a portfolio. Make the ambition readable. Hiring managers value knowing where someone is going, not just where they've been.
4. **Confident layout, no decoration.** Discipline is the aesthetic. Every visual element should serve communication; anything purely decorative comes out. No particles, no gradients-for-texture, no elements that exist to look "designed."
5. **Technical range without mono-culture.** Brandon works across data science, robotics, and web development. The portfolio should feel as comfortable in an ML context as a frontend one — not locked into a single "developer aesthetic."

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline: 4.5:1 contrast for body text, 3:1 for large text. Reduced motion support required (all animations need `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)` alternatives). Full keyboard navigability.
